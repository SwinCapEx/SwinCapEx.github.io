Students will work individually or in teams to address challenges set by academic/industry/community partners, applying their technical knowledge while developing professional skills in teamwork, communication, research, innovation, complex problem solving, digital literacy and self-management.

  • UG_SCI_01 Engineering Baculovirus Vectors for Neuronal and Endothelial Differentiation for Modelling the Blood-Brain Barrier

    Students: Fariha Muskaan, Ash Tormey, Grace Morrissey

    Supervisor: Dr. Huseyin Sumer

    Baculovirus-based gene delivery systems are versatile tools for introducing genes into mammalian cells for applications in stem cell differentiation, reprogramming, and transient expression studies. Unlike traditional viral systems, baculoviruses are non-replicative in mammalian cells and offer high transgene capacity and improved biosafety, making them attractive for research and translational applications. However, efficient monitoring of viral entry and gene delivery remains an important step in optimising these systems. Our laboratory has developed baculoviral plasmid constructs encoding transcription factors used to direct lineage specification, including ETV2 for vascular endothelial differentiation and NGN2 for neuronal differentiation. Together, these factors can be used to generate cell types relevant to the blood–brain barrier (BBB), an important model for neurological research and drug delivery studies. Incorporating a fluorescent reporter into these constructs will enable real-time visualisation and quantification of viral transduction Efficiency.

    Students: Jerusha Venn, Joshua Ward, Roshan Gill

    Supervisor: Tapio Simula

We found the threshold where the driven waves of the system changed from resonant waves to Faraday waves.

  • UG_SCI_03 Electrochemical Carbon Dioxide Reduction Using Copper Electrodes in a Lab-Scale Flow Cell

    Students: Michael Nanni, Ben Gray, Quinn Visoiu

    Supervisor: Associate Professors Peter Mahon & Rosalie Hocking

    In traditional electrochemistry, reactants reach the electrode mainly by diffusion. When an electroactive species is reduced or oxidised at the electrode, a diffusion layer forms near the surface, creating a concentration gradient as reactant concentration falls at the electrode. In stagnant solution, this layer thickens, slowing species transport and making the current diffusion-limited. Flow cells help overcome this bottleneck by using convection to replenish reactants at the electrode surface. This supports carbon dioxide reduction towards valuable feedstocks, especially ethylene, a difficult 12-electron product- an Icarus-like chemical flight towards the sun, where control is needed before the wings melt.

  • UG_SCI_04 Endophytic Fungi from Australian native plants as a source of novel antibiotics

    Students: Zoe Argueso, Cameron Tune, Kira Swaby, Beth Wilde

    Supervisor: Bita Ziferanloo

  • UG_SCI_05 Visualising Gene Delivery During Cellular Reprogramming

    Students: Olivia Pettinella, Olivia Bodegraven, Christina Lenzi

    Supervisor: Dr Huseyin Sumer

    Reprogramming somatic cells into induced pluripotent stems cells (iPSCs) is conducted by introducing Yamanaka factors. This is a relatively new technique that advances regenerative medicine technologies and reduces transplant rejection but currently has a low efficiency rate. Baculoviral gene delivery systems have been used to improve this due to their high transgene capacity. A baculoviral gene delivery plasmid containing Yamanaka factors was modified by Gibson Assembly to insert a red fluorescent protein. This enabled real-time visualisation and quantification of viral transduction efficiency during cellular reprogramming, which is important for monitoring successful gene delivery, assessing reprogramming efficiency, and optimising experimental conditions.

  • UG_SCI_06 Manufacturing Next Generation Nanocarriers Utilising Novel Low-Energy Methods

    Students: Cooper Runting, Ruhalia Mohamed Lareef, Lauren Hills

    Supervisor: Maryam Ghahri

    Effective drug delivery remains a critical challenge in modern medicine, as many therapeutics fail to reach their targets due to degradation, clearance, and membrane impermeability (Abed et al., 2019). Nanocarriers have emerged as promising solutions, improving stability and drug localisation, though conventional carriers relying on passive targeting via the EPR effect show variable clinical outcomes (Danhier, 2016). Cubosomes, lipid-based nanocarriers with a bicontinuous cubic structure, offer superior versatility by encapsulating hydrophilic, lipophilic, and amphiphilic compounds simultaneously (Garg et al., 2007). However, high-energy fabrication methods compromise sensitive cargo. This study aims to develop and characterise a novel low-energy cubosome preparation method, improving consistency while minimising energy consumption and organic solvent use.

  • UG_SCI_09 Copper Polluting our Waterways: Can Native Mint be the Solution?

    Students: Danielle Panzera, Gabrielle Hall, Harry Haszard

    Supervisor: Corey Evans

    Mint Condition Metals explore the potential of native Australian River Mint (Mentha australis) to remove copper contamination from aquatic environments. Heavy metal pollution continues to threaten our waterways and ecosystems, with Australia’s 27 operating copper mines and the remnants of many more leeching copper into our rivers, posing environmental and health risks. This research investigates whether phytoremediation using a native mint species could provide a sustainable, low-cost, and environmentally friendly solution for future water remediation.

  • Category winner

    UG_SCI_10 PANning for the Solution to Your Copper Blues

    Students: Eric Jose, Erin Mayall, David Nguyen

    Supervisor: Daniel Eldridge

    Copper is a well-known heavy metal pollutant that has been found in water sources around the world. This project aims to use polyacrylonitrile (PAN) structure, prepared in 3 separate methods that will then be characterised, functionalised and tested for efficacy as an adsorption material for heavy metals like copper. This project will test the adsorption kinetics and effective pH range for adsorption. Using these properties, the adsorption capacity of functionalised and control structures will be tested to see if they hold up to current industry standards for adsorption materials.
