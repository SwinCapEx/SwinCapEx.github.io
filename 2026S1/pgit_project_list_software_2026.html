  • PG_COS_12 Populating Profiles on a Job Searching Platform from LinkedIn and ORCID by using OAuth and OIDC (11)

    Students: Edinadura Ranushi Mendis, Tuan Hiep Tran, Sreelakshmi Sasikumar Nair, Weihan Zhang, Nawodya Shanthini Rathnayake Rathnayake Mudiyanselage

    Supervisor: Mohammad Sayad Haghighi

    Client: Sheng Wen, CareerQuest

    This project is a realistic simulation for OAuth 2.0 application in practice. A Virtual Private Server hosts a php-based job hunting website (similar to au.jora.com or seek.com.au). The client, who is a Swinburne staff, plays the role of the CEO of the company that owns this job searching platform. The platform is mainly used by job seekers. At the moment, it is using its own proprietary authentication system based on username and password stored in a relational database. For the sake of user convenience and lowering the security risks, the company tends to add a feature to its website to enable users to additionally login with their accounts on well-known identity providers (specifically LinkedIn and ORCID) for user registration and authentication. So new users can register with their e.g. ORCID account in the platform and after that, keep logging into the platform by the same account. In the next step, the client intends to use the information of users at the identity providerís side to enrich their profiles on his platform. For example, users can fetch their qualifications and work experience from LinkedIn/ORCID, or the reputation or badges from Stack Overflow, or the list of software projects from Github, and import them into their profile on this job hunting platform so that they can apply for jobs easier. The client has expressed his interest in the OAuth 2.0 and OpenID Connect technologies to implement these new features. The preferred providrs are ORCID and Stack Overflow, however, LinkedIn and Github can also be used depending on the limitations.

  • PG_COS_13 Integrating OAuth with a Booking Web Application and User KYC Using OCR (12)

    Students: Chalitha Sesath Serasinghe Pathiranage, Kotchamon Jadeekan, Ranveer Narang, Yash Pareshbhai Parekh, Suman Raj Sharma

    Supervisor: Mohammad Sayad Haghighi

    Client: Sheng Wen, ABooking

    This is a simulated project. The students are given a realistic web application hosted on a VPS. The simulated business involves booking rooms for an international hotel via an online platform developed in PHP language. The client, whose role is played by a Swinburne University staff member, acts as the CEO of the company that owns the booking platform. At the moment, the company uses its own in-house-developed authentication system based on usernames and passwords, with credentials stored in a relational database like SQL. However, for the sake of user convenience and to reduce security risks, the company plans to outsource authentication by integrating OAuth 2.0 and OpenID Connect (OIDC) services from a major identity provider (specifically Microsoft, and if not possible, Google) as an additional option for user registration and authentication. This allows new users to register with, for example, their Microsoft email/account and continue logging into the platform with the same account, facilitating user onboarding. However, despite proving email ownership, the user information collected via OAuth 2.0 endpoints (e.g., first name, surname, etc.) is not always reliable. Due to the nature of the business and since customers have different nationalities, to comply with regulations, the client must run Know Your Customer (KYC) procedures to verify identity information and create an authentic customer database. Therefore, the client wants a passport OCR service integrated into their web application. Users/customers can register or log in either in the traditional way or via Microsoft, but they can book (or their booking is confirmed) only after completing the KYC process by uploading a scanned copy of their passport or holding it in front of a mobile/laptop camera for the web application to process and cross check. While many commercial passport OCR services exist, the client prefers turning open-source solutions such as PassportEye and Tesseract (which are readily available in e.g. Python) into a web service.
